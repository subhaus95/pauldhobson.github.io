In April 2020, WestJet's technology team was asked to find eighty million dollars.

Not over a financial year. Not through a structured efficiency programme with a dedicated workstream and a consulting firm attached to it. Immediately, in response to an airline that had gone from carrying millions of passengers a month to flying almost nothing, and that needed to reduce its operating cost base fast enough to survive until it could fly again.

There was no playbook for this. The airline industry had crisis response frameworks for specific failure modes — a fleet grounding, a labour dispute, a volcanic ash cloud closing European airspace for a week. It did not have a framework for the simultaneous collapse of demand across every market, indefinitely, with no recovery timeline. Neither did we.

What followed over the next several months was the most consequential sustained decision-making I have been part of in my career. It was also, in retrospect, the experience that most clearly revealed the difference between crisis management as a concept and crisis management as a practice. The concept is calm, methodical, structured. The practice involves making irreversible decisions at speed under conditions of genuine uncertainty, with incomplete information, while the people you are responsible for are frightened, while the organisation is changing shape around you, and while the operational systems you are cutting the cost of are still, somehow, required to keep working.

The lights stayed on. No system-driven flight delays. No availability failures that compounded the damage the business was already absorbing.

I want to be careful about what I am claiming there. The outcome was good. The process that produced it was not clean.

## What eighty million dollars looks like from the inside

The number sounds like a budget line. It is not a budget line. It is a collection of contracts, people, infrastructure commitments, vendor relationships, and operational dependencies — each of which is load-bearing in some way, each of which has a reason it exists, and none of which was designed to be removed quickly.

The first thing that became clear was that we did not have a complete picture of what we were spending or why. This is not an unusual condition — large technology organisations accumulate cost in ways that are difficult to see clearly because the cost is distributed across teams, vendors, contracts of different vintages, and operational commitments that are not always labelled as technology cost. We knew our major categories. We did not know, with precision, what we would break if we cut any specific thing.

The second thing that became clear was that the distinction between discretionary and non-discretionary cost was much less stable than any normal categorisation suggested. Things that had been treated as fixed turned out to have flexibility, once the question was asked at a level of urgency that had not previously been applied. Things that had been treated as variable turned out to be operationally necessary in ways that had never been surfaced because they had never been threatened.

Both of these are predictable in retrospect. Neither was obvious in the moment.
