When people describe a technology leader as having "broad cross-sector experience," they usually mean it as a compliment without being entirely sure what they are complimenting. The implicit assumption is that exposure to multiple industries produces a kind of accumulated wisdom — that someone who has worked in several different sectors understands something that sector specialists do not.

This is sometimes true and often overstated. The genuine value of cross-sector experience is not breadth in the abstract. It is something more specific: the ability to identify which problems are universal and which are sector-specific — and to recognise, when a sector believes it has a unique problem, whether it is genuinely unique or whether it has been solved somewhere else and not yet imported.

That recognition is harder than it sounds. Every sector believes its constraints are exceptional. Higher education believes its governance complexity is unlike anything in the commercial world. Aviation believes its safety culture and regulatory environment create technology challenges with no commercial parallel. Energy infrastructure believes its combination of operational technology, physical risk, and multi-jurisdictional regulation is simply different in kind from anything outside the industry. Each of these beliefs is partially correct and partially a rationalisation for not looking at solutions that have worked elsewhere.

Having led technology across all three of these sectors over twenty-five years, I can say with some confidence that the beliefs are more wrong than right — and that the most valuable interventions I have made in each sector came from importing something that another sector had already solved.

## What actually transfers

The most reliably transferable things are not technical. They are organisational.

The pattern I have seen repeat across every sector I have worked in is the same: technology functions that have been built to manage and control the technology estate, rather than to enable the organisation to do things it could not do before. The control orientation is understandable — technology carries risk, and risk requires governance. But control-oriented technology functions consistently underdeliver against their potential because they are optimised for preventing the wrong things rather than enabling the right ones.

The shift from a control orientation to an enabling orientation is not a technical change. It is a change in how the function understands its mandate, how it hires, how it measures success, and how it relates to the business. I have made that shift, in different forms, at UBC, at WestJet, and at Plains. The specifics are different in each case. The underlying pattern is identical.

The other reliably transferable thing is the architecture of trust-building in decentralised organisations. Both UBC and WestJet — for entirely different structural reasons — were organisations where the technology function had to operate across a large number of semi-autonomous units that had their own priorities, their own IT capabilities, and their own well-developed scepticism about central technology governance. The tactics differ. The strategic requirement is the same: you earn influence before you need it, you demonstrate value before you claim authority, and you solve the problems people actually have rather than the problems that make your function look good.
