When I left UBC in 2018, after seven and a half years building the enterprise architecture function, the team I left behind did not need me. That sentence is harder to write than it sounds. It is also the only measure of team-building that I now think actually matters.

The temptation in any leadership role is to be necessary. Necessity feels like value. If problems route through you, if decisions require your involvement, if the team's effectiveness is visibly dependent on your presence, then your contribution is legible. You can point to it. The organisation can see what it is getting. The performance review writes itself.

The problem is that a team built around a single person's necessity is not a team. It is a hub-and-spoke arrangement that happens to have job titles. And a hub-and-spoke arrangement fails in two distinct ways: it caps the team's capacity at whatever the hub can personally process, and it creates a succession problem that the organisation typically discovers at the worst possible moment.

I did not start at UBC with a clear theory about this. I started with the opposite instinct — to be the expert, to be the one with the answers, to make myself the reason the function worked. This was not a cynical choice. It was the natural inclination of someone who had built their credibility on technical and organisational knowledge and had not yet understood that deploying that knowledge personally was not the same as building something that would compound.

It took roughly two years to understand that I was building the wrong thing.

## What the wrong thing looked like

The architecture team at UBC in 2011 was not much of a team. The function had existed in name; what it had produced was a collection of technical standards that the university's decentralised faculties and IT units observed when they found it convenient and ignored when they didn't. The governance existed on paper. The influence did not.

The obvious response — the one I initially pursued — was to make the architecture team more authoritative. Better standards. Clearer governance processes. More rigorous review. The assumption was that the problem was insufficient structure, and that sufficient structure would produce compliance.

It produced resentment instead. The faculties did not experience stricter governance as helpful. They experienced it as an overhead imposed by a central function that didn't understand their constraints, their timelines, or their specific needs. The compliance rate improved marginally. The relationship between the architecture function and the people it needed to influence got worse.

The turning point was a conversation with a faculty CIO who told me, with considerable directness, that her team would stop inviting the architecture function into their planning processes if those conversations continued to feel like inspections rather than support. She was not wrong.
