In 2008, the dominant approach to enterprise architecture in UK higher education was ceremonial. Large universities had architecture functions that produced artefacts: reference models, capability maps, standards registers, governance frameworks that described how decisions should be made without meaningfully influencing how they were actually made. The outputs were impressively thorough. The connection to delivery was, in most cases, notional.

This was not unique to higher education. It was the general condition of enterprise architecture as a practice at that point in its development. TOGAF had provided the profession with a comprehensive methodology, and the profession had responded by implementing the methodology rather than solving the underlying problem. The artefacts multiplied. The influence did not.

What we built at Cardiff — and later codified through the JISC Enterprise Architecture Pilot Programme — was an attempt to answer a simpler question: what is the minimum viable architecture practice that actually changes decisions? Not what does a complete architecture function look like, but what, specifically, needs to exist for architectural thinking to have traction in an organisation that will not stop to wait for it?

The answer we arrived at was not what the frameworks suggested. It was leaner, faster, more explicitly political, and considerably less interested in documentation than the prevailing model. It was also, judging by the number of institutions that adopted variants of it through JISC's national dissemination work, more useful.

This is an account of what we did, why we did it, and what it suggests about how architecture practices go wrong — and occasionally right.

## The problem with completeness

The instinct toward completeness is understandable. Enterprise architecture deals with complex, interdependent systems across large organisations, and the temptation is to model that complexity thoroughly before attempting to act on it. If you can see the whole picture clearly enough, the argument goes, the right interventions become obvious.

The problem is that completeness takes time, and organisations do not wait. By the time a comprehensive current-state assessment is finished, the state it describes has already changed. By the time a target architecture has been ratified through governance, the strategic priorities it was designed to serve have shifted. The architecture function is perpetually mapping terrain that has moved, and perpetually producing recommendations for decisions that have already been made.
