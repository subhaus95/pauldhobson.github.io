There is a category of technology that organisations adopt enthusiastically and use badly. Collaboration platforms are a reliable example — SharePoint installed everywhere, used by almost nobody as intended, gradually colonised by the same documents that used to live on file shares. Video conferencing before 2020. Knowledge management systems in general. The technology works. The adoption does not.

Generative AI is following the same pattern, faster and at higher stakes.

In 2023 and 2024, Plains rolled out M365 Copilot across a workforce of more than twelve hundred people spanning two countries and operational contexts that range from corporate finance to pipeline control room support. The technology itself performed largely as advertised. The adoption story was more complicated — and the complications were almost entirely organisational, not technical.

What follows is an account of what actually happened: what we got right, what we misjudged, and what the experience suggests about the difference between deploying AI and adopting it.

## Deployment is not adoption

The distinction matters more than it sounds. Deployment is the technical act of making a capability available. Adoption is the organisational act of changing how work gets done because of it. These are different problems and they require different interventions.

Most organisations are significantly better at deployment than adoption. The infrastructure teams know how to provision, configure, and secure a new platform. The change management capability — the understanding of how to shift working patterns across a population with different roles, different relationships to technology, and different levels of investment in changing how they do things — is usually thinner, less funded, and treated as an afterthought to the technical programme.

With AI tools, the adoption problem is structurally harder than it is with most prior technologies, for a reason that is not immediately obvious. Previous generations of workplace software were task-specific: a project management tool manages projects, a CRM manages customer relationships. The scope of what the tool does is legible. You can train people on it, measure whether they're using it, and connect usage to specific workflows.

A general-purpose AI assistant does not have a defined scope. It is, in principle, useful for almost anything that involves working with language — which is most of what knowledge workers do. That generality is its value proposition and its adoption problem. When a tool can do everything, it is not obvious where to start. People reach for it inconsistently, apply it to the tasks that feel comfortable rather than the tasks where it would create the most leverage, and quietly conclude that it is impressive but not transformative. Which is often true, given how they are using it.
