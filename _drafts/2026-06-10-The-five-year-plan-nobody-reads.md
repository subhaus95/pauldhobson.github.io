Every technology organisation above a certain size produces a long-range plan. It has a horizon — usually five years, sometimes three, occasionally an ambitious ten. It has slides. It has a narrative arc that runs from current state through transition to a target architecture that is, in the diagrams at least, clean and coherent in a way the current state conspicuously is not. It gets presented to a steering committee or a board, it receives approval, and it is then placed somewhere — a SharePoint folder, a strategy repository, a printed copy in a binder on a shelf — where it influences almost nothing that happens next.

This is not cynicism. It is a description of most long-range technology plans, including some I have written.

The reason these plans fail is not that long-range thinking is impossible, or that technology changes too fast for planning to be useful, or that leadership doesn't care about strategy. These are the explanations that get offered in retrospectives, and they are all partially true and mostly beside the point. The real reason is simpler and harder to fix: the plan was written to be approved, not to be used. Those are different documents, and organisations almost always produce the former while believing they are producing the latter.

The first time I saw a strategy conversation change register — actually shift from presenting a position to examining one — was when someone stopped building slides and drew a map instead. Not a roadmap, which is a timeline dressed as a strategy. An actual map: components on two axes, user need at the top, genesis at one end and commodity at the other, with arrows showing the direction everything was moving whether we acted on it or not. The immediate effect was not clarity. It was argument. People who had been nodding at slides started disagreeing about where things actually sat, which meant they were finally engaging with the question the plan was supposed to answer: given where this landscape is going, what should we do?

That argument — uncomfortable, unresolved, nothing like the polished narrative in the approved document — was more strategically useful than anything in the plan it disrupted.
