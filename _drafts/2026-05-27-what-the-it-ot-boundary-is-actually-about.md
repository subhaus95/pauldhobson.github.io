What the IT/OT Boundary Is Actually About

The standard explanation of the IT/OT boundary goes like this: information technology runs on internet time, operational technology runs on industrial time, and the gap between them is a security and integration problem that better tooling will eventually close.

This is accurate as far as it goes. It does not go very far.

The tooling explanation locates the boundary in the wrong place. The seam between IT and OT is not primarily a technical problem — and it is not primarily a time horizon problem either, though that framing gets closer. It is a problem of categorically different consequences. On one side of the boundary, things go wrong and the business suffers. On the other side, things go wrong and people get hurt, pipelines rupture, aircraft don't land correctly, refineries catch fire.

That asymmetry does not just create different risk tolerances. It creates different epistemologies.

The engineers who run operational technology in oil and gas, in aviation, in utilities, are often hard engineers — mechanical, chemical, process, electrical. Their professional formation is built around physical systems where failure has immediate, visible, sometimes irreversible consequences. A control system anomaly is not a degraded user experience. It is a potential loss of containment across eighteen thousand miles of pressurised pipeline. The discipline that emerges from that environment is necessarily conservative, necessarily hierarchical about change, and necessarily sceptical of anything that introduces variability into systems whose behaviour must be predictable under the worst conditions imaginable.

IT culture is built around a different relationship with failure. Agile methodologies treat failure as information. Continuous deployment pipelines are designed to make failure cheap and reversible. The entire intellectual architecture of modern software delivery is premised on the idea that you learn by breaking things in controlled ways. This is not recklessness — it is a rational adaptation to an environment where the consequences of a failed deployment are a rollback and an incident retrospective, not a coroner's report.

These are not just different preferences. They are two professional cultures with coherent internal logic, each shaped by the failure modes of its own environment — and they have almost no common language for talking about risk.
