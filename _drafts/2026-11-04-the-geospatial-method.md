In the mid-1990s, the MODIS instrument aboard NASA's Terra satellite was producing roughly a terabyte of raw data per day. This was, at the time, an almost incomprehensible volume — more than most organisations would process in a year, arriving continuously, covering the entire surface of the Earth every one to two days. The scientific value was potentially enormous. The practical challenge was that most of the data was noise: cloud cover, atmospheric interference, sensor artefacts, geometric distortion from the curvature of the Earth and the angle of the satellite's pass. The signal — the actual surface reflectance data that could tell you something about land cover, vegetation health, snow and ice extent — was embedded in all of that noise, and extracting it required algorithms that could distinguish the two at scale, automatically, without human review of every pixel.

The work I did during those years at the Swansea University Earth Observation group was, in its essentials, the design and validation of methods for finding signal in large, noisy datasets. Not the signal that was obvious. The signal that was recoverable if you understood the structure of the noise well enough to model it and subtract it.

I have been doing the same work ever since, in different domains. The datasets are organisational rather than geophysical. The noise is political, cultural, historical — the accumulated decisions, relationships, incentive structures, and informal rules that shape how an organisation actually behaves as opposed to how it officially describes itself. The signal is what the organisation is actually trying to do, what is actually blocking it, and what, specifically, needs to change for it to work better.

This is not a metaphor. It is a method, and it transfers more directly than it might appear.

## What the noise actually is

Every organisation generates more information about itself than it can consciously process. There are formal signals — strategy documents, performance reports, project status updates, budget variances — that describe the organisation as it would like to be seen. There are informal signals — the conversations that happen before meetings, the decisions that get made and then unmade, the projects that are officially green and operationally troubled, the people who are nominally in charge of things and the people who are actually in charge of them — that describe the organisation as it actually is.

The formal signals are not false, exactly. They are selectively accurate. They reflect the parts of the organisation's reality that are legible in formal reporting structures, that have found their way into the metrics, that are comfortable to surface. The informal signals carry the rest — the problems that are real but not yet named, the constraints that are accepted as fixed but are not, the capabilities that exist but are invisible because they belong to people whose status in the hierarchy doesn't reflect their actual contribution.

An organisation that is only reading its formal signals is like a satellite only processing the cloud-free pixels. The image looks clean. It is also missing most of the information.

The work of understanding an organisation — really understanding it, in a way that allows you to intervene effectively — requires building a model of both signal types and understanding how they relate to each other. Where do the formal and informal signals diverge? The divergence is almost always where the interesting problems are. Where does the informal signal suggest something is possible that the formal signal says is fixed? That gap is where the intervention opportunities live.
