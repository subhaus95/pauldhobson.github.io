In March 2020, WestJet grounded most of its fleet. Within six weeks, the airline went from carrying millions of passengers across 100 destinations to flying almost nothing. The technology team had to cut $80 million in operating costs — immediately, without notice, without a recovery timeline — while keeping the systems running that the airline needed to survive and eventually rebuild.
We did it. No system-driven flight delays. No availability failures that compounded an already catastrophic situation. The lights stayed on while we turned most of them off.

I have been asked a number of times since then what the team did to make that possible. The honest answer is: not much, in March 2020. The work that made the pandemic response possible was done in 2018 and 2019, when nobody was thinking about pandemics, when the business was growing, when cost reduction was not the question anyone was asking. The crisis revealed an architecture. It did not create one.

This is the thing that is hardest to explain about resilience in complex technology environments. The decisions that determine whether an organisation can absorb a shock are almost never made during the shock. They are made months or years earlier, usually in conversations about something else entirely — a contract renewal, a cloud migration business case, a decision about how to structure a vendor relationship. At the time, those decisions look like ordinary operational choices. In retrospect, they are the architecture.

The difficulty is that ordinary operational choices do not look like strategic investments in future resilience. They look like cost lines and delivery commitments and risk registers. The language organisations use to make these decisions is almost entirely focused on the immediate — what does this cost now, what does it deliver by Q3, what is the implementation risk. The question of what this makes possible under conditions that do not currently exist is rarely part of the formal evaluation. It is rarely even articulated.
