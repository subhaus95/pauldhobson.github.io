The customer-centricity frameworks that dominate management thinking were built for organisations that sell things. They assume a relatively legible relationship between the organisation and the people whose money it is trying to earn — a relationship that can be studied, measured, and optimised. Voice of the customer programmes. Net Promoter Scores. Customer journey mapping. These tools are useful when the customer is identifiable, when their preferences can be surveyed, and when the organisation's success is directly tied to satisfying them.

Internal technology leaders do not have customers in this sense. The people they serve are colleagues, not paying clients. The consequences of failure are operational rather than commercial in the direct sense — a system outage does not produce customer churn in a form that shows up in a revenue report, even when it absolutely should. The relationship is one of internal service provision, with all the political and incentive complications that entails.

This creates a real problem, but the right response to it is not to import the external customer framework wholesale and apply it to internal stakeholders while pretending the differences don't matter. That produces technology functions that talk about "customers" when they mean the finance team, that conduct "customer satisfaction surveys" to measure how people feel about the service desk, and that use the language of customer-centricity as a posture while remaining fundamentally inward-facing in how they make decisions.

The failure mode the customer-first framing is trying to address is genuine. Technology functions that define success primarily in terms of their own operational metrics — uptime, delivery velocity, budget adherence — can perform well against those metrics while providing poor service to the organisation. The metrics measure the function's health, not its value. The fix is not the language. It is a change in whose problem you are actually trying to solve.

## The right customer

The most useful reframe for internal technology leaders is not "treat the business as your customer." It is: who is the end user of the system, and is that person's experience visible to you?

In a pipeline company, the end user of an operational technology system is the control room operator monitoring pipeline pressure across eighteen thousand miles of infrastructure, at three in the morning, in conditions where an anomaly that goes undetected has physical consequences. That person's experience of the system — not the technology team's assessment of its uptime, not the vendor's performance metrics, not the project manager's RAG status — is the signal that determines whether the technology is doing its job.

In an airline, the end user of a crew management system is the dispatcher trying to reassemble a day's schedule after a weather event has made the original plan inoperative. The check-in agent managing a gate full of passengers whose connection is at risk. The engineer whose ability to clear an aircraft for departure depends on maintenance records being accurate and accessible. These are the people whose working experience reflects whether the technology estate is actually fit for purpose.

In a university, the end user of the student information system is the student trying to register for courses during the one-week window before classes start, and the advisor trying to help them when something goes wrong. Not the IT team's ticketing system. Not the vendor's SLA report.

The gap between these end users and the technology function is often large, structural, and invisible. It is invisible because the feedback loops that would make it visible — the ones that would surface the control room operator's experience to the people making technology investment decisions — do not exist by default. They have to be built deliberately.
